The Reaction

From a secondhand PC at 12 to multi-market campaigns across Southeast Asia.

~2001 · Age 12

A secondhand PC and a lot of free time

My first computer was a used one — slow, unstable, and constantly getting viruses. At the same time, I spent a lot of hours at internet cafes playing games and just browsing, back when the internet still felt like a new thing. When the PC problems got bad enough, I started looking into fixing them myself. By 13, I had taken a PC assembly course and could disassemble, reassemble, and reinstall Windows on my own. I even built a small VBS-based virus during that course, mostly out of curiosity. After that, I was unofficially the "computer person" among my friends. But I also genuinely liked chemistry.

2009 · SMAKBO

Best chemistry vocational school in Indonesia — and I still did web design on the side

Before high school, I had to choose: a regular academic track, a computer vocational school, or a chemistry one. I sat tests for all of them. Didn't get into the regular public school. But I got into SMAKBO — the top chemistry analysis vocational school in Indonesia. The chemistry track won by default, but I kept finding ways to stay close to computers. I joined the Web Design extracurricular and usually ranked near the top in computer subjects (everything else was pretty average, not going to lie). That gap between what I was studying and what I kept gravitating toward didn't quite go away.

2012–2022 · Lab Years

Ten years in the lab. The IT department wasn't winning.

After SMAKBO I went straight to work — lab analyst roles across food and beverage, dairy, and environmental testing. For a decade I analyzed things that aren't visible to the naked eye: metal content in food, nutritional profiles in dairy products, toxins in water samples. It trained me to care about data accuracy and to document everything properly. At one point, my supervisor mentioned — more than once — that my computer knowledge was better than the company's IT staff. I didn't take it too seriously at first, but between that and multiple classmates telling me I was in the wrong field, I started paying more attention.

2016–2019 · First real steps

Communities, web projects, and running Meta Ads without knowing what I was doing

In 2016 I started taking digital more seriously — joining online communities, showing up to offline meetups, getting to know people already working in the space. By 2018 I was part of a small remote team building websites for clients across Indonesia. I wasn't doing the coding — more project coordination and admin — but I was learning the whole time. In 2019 I ran my first Meta Ads campaign for a side business. Looking back, I had no idea what I was doing. No structure, no attribution, basically guessing. But I kept going anyway.

2020 · Covid

Everything stopped at once

When the pandemic hit, all client work dried up overnight. A home decor side project I had been running for the US market also had to stop — supply chains from China shut down and there was no path forward. I went back to focusing on my day job, still in the lab, still in school, still on shift work. But I kept watching what was happening around me. Covid was forcing everything toward digital, faster than anyone had planned. I filed that away.

2022 · The turn

RevoU, and finally doing this properly

Once I finished my degree and moved to a job without shift hours, the window opened. I enrolled in RevoU's Full Stack Digital Marketing bootcamp — 200+ hours of structured training. For the first time, all the pieces I had been picking up over the years — the data instincts from the lab, the platform tinkering, the community contacts — had a proper framework around them. That's where the professional side of this started.

10 Years in the Lab + RevoU → Performance Marketer

Now

From local SMEs — clinics, catering, car rentals, travel agencies, fashion, skincare — to larger brands running campaigns across multiple countries. I still think of this as early in a much longer run that started when I was 12 years old.
